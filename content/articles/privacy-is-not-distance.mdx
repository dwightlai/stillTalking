When a child was young, parents needed to know where they were, who they were with, and whether something was wrong. That habit does not disappear automatically when the child becomes an adult.

But the purpose of parenting changes. Safety monitoring must gradually become adult-to-adult trust.

Technology can keep the old role alive. In a 2023 Pew survey, one in four parents said they tracked an adult child’s location at least occasionally. The age difference was sharp: 42% said so when answering about an 18- to 24-year-old, compared with 9% when answering about a child in their early 30s. The numbers do not tell us whether consent was given. They do show how easily access can continue after the reason for automatic access has changed.

## Less information can mean a healthier boundary

Adult children may stop sharing details because every update becomes an evaluation. A casual mention of work turns into career advice. A new relationship becomes a list of warnings. A weekend plan becomes a debate about money.

Withholding information may not be punishment. It may be the simplest way to keep an ordinary choice from becoming a family argument.

## Replace access with invitation

Instead of asking five detailed questions, try one open invitation:

“How are you feeling about everything lately?”

Then let the answer be as short or as long as your child chooses.

## Try saying this

“You do not have to tell me everything for me to care about you. If you want to talk, I am here.”

This does not mean a parent must ignore serious safety concerns. It means everyday curiosity should not be treated as entitlement.

## Privacy is not secrecy by default

Secrecy implies that information is being hidden to deceive someone who has a legitimate claim to it. Privacy is the ordinary right to decide who knows what about your life.

An adult child may keep a medical appointment, disagreement with a partner, salary, travel plan, or early career idea private while they understand it themselves. That is not necessarily a statement about the parent’s importance.

Parents sometimes respond to missing information by searching for it elsewhere: asking siblings, checking social media activity, reading location data, or questioning the partner. Even when the information seems harmless, bypassing the child’s choice confirms that direct boundaries will not be respected.

If you learn something indirectly, do not use it to stage an interrogation. Let the person decide whether and when to tell you.

## Make sharing emotionally safer

Adult children often ration information because of what happens after disclosure.

They mention a new job possibility and receive daily follow-ups. They share a minor relationship problem and hear criticism of the partner for months. They describe stress and the parent becomes so alarmed that the child must provide reassurance.

Safety in conversation is built through response:

- Ask whether the person wants listening, questions, or ideas.
- Do not retell private news to relatives without permission.
- Avoid making one update evidence of a permanent pattern.
- Let uncertainty remain unresolved for a while.
- Do not punish honesty with “I knew this would happen.”

The reward for receiving information safely is not guaranteed access. It is a greater chance that sharing will remain voluntary.

## When parents genuinely need information

Some details are shared responsibilities rather than private preferences. If your child lives in your home, uses your car, depends on a joint insurance plan, or has asked you to act in an emergency, you may need specific information.

Name the practical reason and limit the request to what is necessary:

> “Because the car is in our name, we need to know who is insured to drive it. We do not need a report on where you go.”

This is different from using practical involvement to justify broad access.

If there is a credible safety concern, be direct about the observation. “You have missed work for a week and said you do not feel safe alone” is a reason to discuss immediate support. “You have been private lately” is not, by itself, proof of danger.

## Responding to “I don’t want to talk about it”

The healthiest immediate answer is often simple:

> “Okay. I am here if that changes.”

Avoid sighing, withdrawing affection, or asking again through another family member. Respecting a small no is how you demonstrate that larger conversations can be trusted.

You are still allowed to have feelings about the distance. Share those feelings without turning them into a demand: “I miss knowing more about your life” is honest. “After everything we have done, you owe us an explanation” converts closeness into debt.

## What to do next

Notice the next time your child shares a small piece of news. Before offering an opinion, ask one curious question. A conversation grows when information is received safely.
